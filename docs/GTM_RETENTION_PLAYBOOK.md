# TuGymPR — GTM & Retention Service Playbook

> Working thesis for selling TuGymPR as a **retention service**, not a fitness app.
> Drafted 2026-05-24. Living document — sharpen with real gym data.

---

## 1. The core thesis (the one sentence)

**We make your members feel like the gym gives a damn — at scale, without you lifting a finger.**

TuGymPR is sold as a **churn-reduction service**, where the software is the engine and Leonel (at first) is the human layer. The product is not features — it's the *outcome*: members stay, and members feel cared for.

---

## 2. The market wedge (why this works in PR)

- **No gym here does retention.** Nobody calls you when you stop showing up. Nobody asks why you left. Nobody welcomes you back. Gyms compete on price, machines, location, hours — never on *caring*.
- This is **uncontested space**. You're not competing on features or price; you're competing on a feeling nobody else delivers.
- It's **defensible, not just first-mover**, because gyms can't easily copy it. Doing it requires two things they lack:
  1. **The data** to know who's slipping — ~95% of gyms don't track churn and literally don't know who's about to leave.
  2. **The time/role** — front desk is busy collecting, cleaning, attending. Nobody owns retention.
- TuGymPR brings **both at once**: software that sees who's leaving + a person who acts on it. To copy you, a gym would have to build the tracking AND dedicate a full-time person. They won't.

**Pitch gut-punch to the owner:** *"I bet you can't tell me the name of one member who quit last month, or why."* They won't know. That's the moment the ficha drops.

> ⚠️ The ~9–10% monthly churn figure for PR is an **assumption** borrowed from US (Hormozi) numbers — unvalidated. Don't assert it to a gym ("your churn is 10%" → "no it isn't" → you lose). Instead lead with: *"do you even know your churn right now?"* — sell **visibility first**, dashboard second. Validate the real number with early clients.

---

## 3. The product = software + service (done-for-you)

The differentiator is the **DFY layer**. Software alone is copyable; *"Leonel calls your at-risk members and brings them back"* is not.

**Two retention motions:**

| Motion | What it is | Speed | Who does it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Preventive save** | App flags members going cold (declining check-ins, churn score). Leonel calls *before* they cancel. | Ongoing drip | Software tracks → Leonel calls |
| **Win-back blitz** | Call everyone who left in the last ~5 years. Ask why they left (data!), make a "come home" offer. | Front-loaded burst | Leonel |

**Key timing insight:** the exit interview is *too late* if you wait for a formal cancellation — they checked out mentally weeks earlier. The money is in catching the **behavioral signal before they cancel**, which is exactly what the churn score does.

---

## 4. The offer / guarantee

**"In the first ~2–3 months we get you enough attributable recovery members that the service pays for itself. After that, it's pure profit."**

- The **5-year win-back list is the engine** for hitting this fast — it's a finite pile of warm, already-knew-you prospects you can work through immediately for quick, named wins. (Preventive saves are slower; the blitz delivers the early proof.)
- **Attribution is the trap.** Churn is noisy month-to-month — don't promise to "lower churn %," promise **visible, attributable saves**: *"these 12 were flagged / called, here's who's still here paying."* A list with names, not a fuzzy statistic. The DFY model makes attribution airtight (Leonel made the call, logged the result).
- **Use 90 days, not 60**, for the guarantee window — 60 is too tight for noisy data. Structure: *"If we don't get enough attributable recovery members to cover what you paid in 90 days, the next month is free."* Risk reversal without giving away everything.
- **Count returnees at their real (discounted) value** in ROI. Report conservative, over-deliver.
- **Measure the baseline first** (month 0). Without it, the guarantee can't be proven either way.
- **Don't promise 3% churn.** That's Hormozi's best case *with* full coaching systems, not an app alone. Promise "a meaningful, measured reduction" and let data prove it. Under-promise, over-deliver.

## 5. Pricing — the 3-tier ladder

The ladder itself sells (price anchoring: the $1,000 tier makes $299 look cheap).

| Tier | ~Price | What they get |
|---|---|---|
| **Software only** | ~$299/mo | They run it themselves. For the gym that wants to DIY. |
| **Done-with-you** | mid | Software + Leonel trains their staff to use it. |
| **Done-for-you** | **$1,000+/mo** | Leonel runs the whole retention program: preventive save calls, win-back blitz, exit interviews, brings printed cards to the gym for staff to hand out. They pay more, get the full machine. |

(Plus setup fee — factor it into the "pays for itself" math: ~$2–3K setup + monthly = payback target.)

**Why DFY first / do it yourself at the start:**
- If you depend on the gym to implement, they won't, it fails, and *your software* gets blamed for their non-adoption. Doing it yourself removes that variable and **guarantees the first clients are winners** → which become the case studies that sell everyone else.
- "Do things that don't scale" — you don't have 1,000 clients, you need to win the first 3. By hand is how.
- Every manual call is **R&D**: which member to call, what worked, which offer landed, what objection came up — that becomes the product's intelligence and your future playbook.

**The scaling reality (not a problem now, but on the radar):** DFY doesn't scale with you alone — ~5–10 hrs/gym/month of calls means a ceiling of ~5–8 DFY gyms (~$8K/mo) before you're maxed. At ~5 gyms, decide: train someone on your playbook, or stay boutique and raise price. **Document everything as you go** so the knowledge isn't trapped in your head — that's the difference between a permanent ceiling and being able to delegate.

## 6. Offer philosophy — relational, NOT the broken discount framework

- **"First month free/cheap to a stranger" is the broken framework** every gym uses. It attracts price-shoppers with no commitment; what's gotten cheap isn't valued, they don't show, they churn when full price hits. Wrong customer, and it trains the market to wait for discounts.
- **Win-back is the opposite:** you're not buying a stranger, you're reactivating a *relationship that already existed*. The discount isn't the hook — it's **permission to come back without losing face.** People don't return for 25% off; they return because they *belonged* and someone remembered them. *"Vuelve a casa, ya eras de los nuestros."*
- **The discount is the threshold, not the reason to stay.** They stay because (a) you acknowledged/fixed why they left, and (b) the experience gives them a new reason to belong. If it's only the discount, they re-churn.
- **Prefer value over price cuts.** A discount says "we were overpriced"; a bonus says "here's extra." Lean toward a **one-time "welcome home" gesture** (a bonus month at the end, a PT session, gym merch) over an **ongoing** discount that bleeds revenue all year and trains bad behavior. Same emotional effect, better economics.
- **The gym pre-approves the offer menu** before any blitz — you're discounting *their* revenue, so agree on a menu so you can make offers live on calls.
- **Call as the gym** ("calling from [Gym]"), with the owner's blessing and member list — it's their brand recovering their people, not a stranger cold-calling.

## 7. Why they STAY this time — the value stack

The call re-acquires them (emotional re-entry). The **experience retains them** (closes the re-churn risk). Each feature attacks a *specific* reason people quit — not random features:

| Feature | Churn cause it kills |
|---|---|
| App removes "what do I train / eat today?" | **Decision fatigue** — the silent #1 killer of the habit. People quit because thinking is friction, not from laziness. |
| Progress tracking (charts, 1RM, PRs) | People quit when they **can't see results**. Show progress → they stay. |
| Points & rewards for attending / buying at gym | **Sunk cost** — they don't want to lose accumulated points → they come back even on low-motivation days. |
| Challenges + community + live training indicator | **Belonging + social accountability** — hard to leave a place where you feel you belong. |
| Custom cards (milestones, birthdays, welcome-back) | **Feeling seen** — the personal touch nobody else gives. |

**North star for the member experience: it must feel *effortless*.** The moment the app feels like homework, you've lost. The keyword is *"despreocuparse"* — the app should make being fit feel *handled*, not like one more thing to manage.

**"Appears more attentive but underneath it's a service" is not deception — it's operational excellence.** The care is real (real call, real rewards, real community); the service layer is just the engine that makes genuine care *consistent and scalable* instead of depending on the staff's mood that day.

## 8. Unit economics (illustrative — replace with real gym data)

Example: 300 members, ~$40/mo average fee.

- 10% monthly churn = **30 members lost/mo** = $1,200/mo = **$14,400/yr** walking out the door. ← *the headline number to sell*
- Saved members are **recurring + compounding**: save ~15/mo and each keeps paying →
  - Mo 1: +$600/mo · Mo 2: +$1,200/mo · Mo 3: +$1,800/mo … Mo 12: +$7,200/mo
  - First-year cumulative retained revenue ≈ **$40K+** (optimistic; assumes saves don't re-churn — report conservative).
- **The ROI close:** charge ~$299–$1,000+/mo; save them conservatively $7–14K/yr → **2–4x ROI minimum.** When the ROI is that obvious, you're not selling software — you're selling a $1 bill for 50 cents.

## 9. The proof asset

Your best sales weapon comes out of the calls: the **returned member's own testimonial** — *"I came back because someone from the gym actually called me. Nobody had ever done that."* Those words, in a member's mouth, sell harder than any dashboard.

Capture these from day one.
